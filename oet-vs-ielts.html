What is OET?

The Occupational English Test is designed specifically for healthcare professionals.

OET was developed in Australia in 1988 and is now recognised by healthcare regulators, immigration authorities, and universities across 12 countries. Unlike general English tests, every aspect of OET is set in a healthcare context — the reading passages are medical journal extracts, the listening tasks are clinical consultations, and the writing task requires you to produce a referral or discharge letter.

This design makes OET more relevant for healthcare professionals but also means you need strong clinical vocabulary and an understanding of healthcare communication conventions, not just general English ability.

OET is available for 12 healthcare professions, each with a profession-specific writing and speaking sub-test: nursing, medicine, dentistry, pharmacy, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietetics, radiography, speech pathology, optometry, podiatry, and veterinary science.

OET advantage

Because the writing task uses clinical scenarios, strong clinicians often find OET writing easier than an IELTS essay — you're doing something you already do at work, just in more formal English. If you're confident in your clinical knowledge, this works in your favour.

What is IELTS?

IELTS Academic is the world's most widely taken English proficiency test.

IELTS is jointly owned by the British Council, IDP Australia, and Cambridge Assessment English. It's taken by over 3.5 million people annually and accepted by over 10,000 organisations in 140 countries — including universities, employers, and immigration authorities.

For healthcare professionals, IELTS Academic (not IELTS General Training) is the relevant version. The Academic version assesses the English skills needed for undergraduate and postgraduate study environments — reading academic texts, writing essays and reports, and discussing abstract topics.

The content is entirely non-medical. A nurse preparing for IELTS might read a passage about climate change, write an essay about urban planning, and discuss abstract social topics in the speaking test. For some healthcare professionals, this disconnect from clinical practice makes IELTS preparation feel harder despite it being a less specialised test.

Important

Do not confuse IELTS Academic with IELTS General Training. Healthcare regulators and most universities require IELTS Academic. IELTS General Training is primarily for secondary education and work experience applications in the UK, Canada, and Australia.

Scoring and Requirements Compared

Understanding what scores you actually need.

OET Scoring

OET uses letter grades: A (highest), B, C+, C, D, E. Most healthcare regulators require a minimum of Grade B in all four sub-tests. Grade B roughly corresponds to a high level of professional English — able to communicate effectively in most clinical situations.

The Writing sub-test is often the most challenging. It is graded on criteria including purpose, content, conciseness, layout, and language — with language quality being the most heavily weighted criterion.

IELTS Scoring

IELTS uses a 0–9 band scale in 0.5 increments. Most healthcare regulators require band 7.0 in all four sub-tests (not just an overall average). This is a high standard — band 7 means "good user" with occasional inaccuracies and misunderstandings.

Some regulators accept an overall score of 7.0 with no sub-test below 6.5 or 7.0. Check your specific regulator's requirements carefully, as these vary.

Score equivalence

OET Grade B is broadly equivalent to IELTS 7.0. OET Grade A is broadly equivalent to IELTS 8.0 or above. However, the two tests measure somewhat different aspects of English ability, so direct equivalence is approximate.

Which Test is Harder?

The honest answer depends on your background and strengths.

OET is harder if you:

  • Have limited clinical experience in English-speaking environments
  • Are unfamiliar with the format of clinical letters (referral, discharge, transfer)
  • Struggle with medical spelling and clinical vocabulary
  • Find role-play scenarios with simulated patients stressful

IELTS is harder if you:

  • Have strong clinical English but weaker general academic English
  • Find abstract essay writing challenging
  • Are not used to reading non-medical academic texts at speed
  • Struggle with the graph/chart description task in IELTS Writing Task 1

In practice, most healthcare professionals who take both tests report that OET feels more natural because the content matches their daily work. However, IELTS preparation materials are more widely available, cheaper, and the test is offered more frequently — which matters if you need to sit multiple attempts.

Which Test Should You Choose?

A practical decision framework based on your situation.

Choose OET if:

  • Your target country/regulator accepts OET (UK, Australia, NZ, Ireland)
  • You have strong clinical English and are comfortable with medical scenarios
  • You find abstract academic writing challenging
  • You want to practice skills that directly transfer to your clinical work
  • You are a nurse, doctor, or allied health professional with clinical experience in English

Choose IELTS if:

  • Your target country primarily accepts IELTS (USA, Canada, Gulf region)
  • You also need the qualification for university admission or immigration purposes
  • OET is not available in your area or is too expensive for multiple attempts
  • You have strong general English but limited clinical English vocabulary
From personal experience

As a doctor who has gone through the OET preparation process, my recommendation for most healthcare professionals targeting the UK or Australia is OET. The clinical context makes the writing task genuinely useful — the skills you develop preparing for OET Writing will make you a better clinical communicator, which IELTS preparation will not. The spelling and vocabulary practice transfers directly to your daily work.
