Why Spelling Still Matters in a Spellcheck World

It is tempting to think autocorrect has made spelling optional. It hasn't, and the reason is about how the brain learns to read and write, not about typos.

Children who spell confidently read more fluently. The two skills share the same underlying knowledge: how letters map to sounds, how words are built from smaller parts, and how families of words follow shared patterns. When a child learns to spell national, nation and nationality, they are not memorising three separate strings of letters, they are learning that related words keep a shared root. That knowledge feeds straight back into reading comprehension and vocabulary.

Spelling also carries real weight in school assessment. Most curricula test spelling directly through weekly lists and dictation, and indirectly through the accuracy marks in every piece of writing. A child who spells accurately writes faster, because they are not stopping to second-guess every other word. That freed-up attention goes into ideas, structure and expression, the things that actually earn marks.

None of this requires hours of drilling. It requires the right kind of short, regular practice, which is what the rest of this guide is about.

How Schools Actually Test Spelling

Before you practise, it helps to know what you are practising for. Almost every school spelling test works the same way, and it is worth copying that format at home:

  • The word is spoken aloud. The teacher says the word, often in a sentence for context, and the child writes it from memory.
  • There is no word on screen to copy. The child has to retrieve the spelling, not recognise it. This is the crucial difference from most spelling apps, which show the word and ask the child to tap letters, a far easier task that does not build real recall.
  • Accuracy is all-or-nothing. A single wrong letter usually means the whole word is marked incorrect.

This tells you exactly how to practise: your child should hear the word, then write or type it from memory, then check. Practice that only involves reading a list, or copying words while looking at them, feels productive but rarely transfers to the test. The gap between "I recognise this word" and "I can produce this word from memory" is where most marks are lost.

The One Method That Works: Look, Say, Cover, Write, Check

If you take only one thing from this guide, take this. It is the method most teachers recommend, and it works because it forces active recall.

For each word, your child does five steps in order:

  1. Look, look carefully at the word and notice its tricky part (the silent letter, the double letter, the unexpected vowel).
  2. Say, say the word aloud, and say it in a way that helps, for example sounding out Wed-nes-day for Wednesday.
  3. Cover, cover the word completely so it cannot be seen.
  4. Write, write the word from memory.
  5. Check, uncover and compare letter by letter. If it is wrong, do not just move on, repeat all five steps for that word.

The power is in the cover step. The moment the word is hidden, the brain has to reconstruct it, and reconstruction is what builds durable memory. Copying a visible word skips this and teaches almost nothing. A digital version of this method simply speaks the word aloud (nothing to copy), the child types it from memory, and the app checks each letter instantly, the same five steps, faster feedback.

Spelling Patterns Children Find Hardest

Most spelling mistakes are not random. They cluster around a handful of predictable patterns. Teaching the pattern is far more efficient than teaching one word at a time, because the pattern unlocks dozens of words at once.

1. Silent letters

Letters that are written but not heard: the k in knee and knight, the b in lamb and thumb, the w in wrist and write, the h in hour and honest. The fix is to over-pronounce them in practice: say "k-nee" aloud to lock in the hidden letter.

2. Double letters

Words like necessary, accommodation, beginning and occurred trip up adults, let alone children. A useful rule for many words: when adding -ing or -ed to a short word ending in a single vowel plus consonant, double the consonant, run → running, stop → stopped.

3. The schwa (the lazy vowel)

In unstressed syllables the vowel collapses into a soft "uh" sound, so children cannot hear which letter to write, the a in separate, the o in doctor, the e in different. The trick is to say the word in an exaggerated way, "sep-A-rate", so the hidden vowel becomes audible.

4. "i before e" and its exceptions

The old rule "i before e except after c" holds for believe, field and receive, but breaks for weird, seize and their. Teach the rule, but also teach the common exceptions as their own small group.

5. Suffixes that change the root

Adding -ful, -ly, -ness or -tion often changes the ending: happy → happiness, beauty → beautiful. The pattern "change y to i" covers a huge number of words.

What Words to Practise, by Stage

Practise words that are slightly above what your child already knows, familiar enough to attempt, hard enough to stretch. As a rough guide:

Early primary (ages 5–7)

High-frequency words and simple patterns: said, come, some, there, were, because, friend, people, school, would. These appear constantly in everyday writing, so accuracy here pays off immediately.

Middle primary (ages 7–9)

Longer words and clear patterns: beautiful, favourite, February, different, remember, separate, business, category, exercise, February, plus common homophones like there / their / they're and to / too / two.

Upper primary (ages 9–11)

Curriculum words that reliably appear in national spelling lists: accommodate, conscience, definite, embarrass, environment, necessary, occasion, possession, recommend, rhythm. This is the tier where the tricky patterns above matter most.

Because every school and region uses a slightly different list, the most useful practice uses your child's own class list. A tool that lets you type in the ten words your child was given this week, and then speaks them aloud for practice, matches the actual test far better than any generic list.

A Simple 15-Minute Weekly Routine

Little and often beats one long cramming session. Spaced practice across the week produces far better recall than the same total time the night before the test.

  1. Monday, introduce the week's ten words. Do one round of look, say, cover, write, check. Note which words were wrong.
  2. Wednesday, practise only the words that were wrong on Monday, plus a quick run through all ten.
  3. Friday, a full mock test: you read each word aloud, your child writes it from memory, then check together.

Three sessions of five minutes will out-perform thirty minutes of copying. Keep a short record of which words keep coming back as errors, those are the ones worth carrying into next week.

Making It Stick Without the Stress

Spelling practice goes wrong when it becomes a battle. A few principles keep it positive:

  • Keep sessions short. Stop before your child is tired. Five focused minutes beats fifteen reluctant ones.
  • Praise the process, not just the score. "You spotted the silent letter" is more useful than "good, ten out of ten".
  • Treat mistakes as information. A wrong word is simply the next thing to learn, not a failure. Re-do it calmly and move on.
  • Make feedback instant. Children learn fastest when they find out immediately whether a word was right, while the attempt is still fresh. This is one area where a well-designed app genuinely helps, it checks each letter the moment the word is typed.
  • Let them hear the word. Reading a word off a page is a different skill from spelling it from sound. Practice should start with the spoken word.
