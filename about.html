Why SpellRightPro Exists

I am a practising doctor who needed to pass OET to continue my medical career in an English-speaking country. I had strong clinical knowledge. I had years of experience. What I struggled with was one specific, solvable problem: spelling medical words correctly under exam conditions.

The words I had been abbreviating in clinical notes for years — hypertension, haemorrhage, electrocardiogram, dyspnoea — suddenly needed to be spelled in full, correctly, in a formal letter, under timed exam pressure. My clinical brain knew what they meant. My fingers couldn't always produce the right letters.

I searched for a tool that would let me practise OET medical spelling specifically — listen to a word spoken in a British accent, type it from memory, get immediate feedback, and track which words I kept getting wrong. I found general spelling apps that weren't medical. I found OET preparation courses that weren't focused on spelling. I found word lists I could read but not practise with.

So I built SpellRightPro.

The insight behind the tool

Recognising a correctly spelled word when you see it is much easier than producing it from memory. Most spelling resources — lists, flashcards, reading — only train recognition. SpellRightPro trains production: you hear the word, you write it from memory, you get immediate feedback. That is how the OET exam works, and that is how effective spelling practice works.

Who SpellRightPro Is For

SpellRightPro was designed primarily for healthcare professionals preparing for OET — nurses, doctors, pharmacists, physiotherapists, and other allied health professionals who need to demonstrate English proficiency to register or work in an English-speaking country.

Over time, it has also become useful for:

  • School students — practising curriculum spelling lists with the same listen-and-type method
  • Spelling Bee competitors — practising with voice recognition so they can spell words aloud, just as they would in competition
  • Healthcare educators — creating custom word lists for student assessments
  • Non-native English speakers in clinical settings — consolidating medical vocabulary spelling for professional writing
