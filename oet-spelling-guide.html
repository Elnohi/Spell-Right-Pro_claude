Why Spelling Matters in OET

Many OET candidates underestimate spelling — and it costs them band scores they deserve.

In the OET Writing sub-test, examiners assess your ability to communicate clinical information clearly and accurately. Misspelled medical terms create ambiguity in a real clinical context — "haemorrhage" spelled as "hemorrhge" could be misread in a referral letter, with serious consequences. OET examiners mark accordingly.

In the OET Listening sub-test (Parts A, B, and C), you transcribe what you hear onto forms and into notes. Spelling errors on patient names, drug names, dosages, and diagnoses directly cost marks — even if you heard the information correctly.

Key insight

Unlike IELTS, OET uses real healthcare scenarios. The vocabulary is drawn from clinical practice — which means the words are longer, less common in everyday English, and frequently borrowed from Latin or Greek. This makes spelling significantly harder for non-native speakers.

Research from OET preparation providers consistently shows that spelling errors cluster around a predictable set of words. The 80 words in this guide account for the majority of spelling errors seen in OET Writing scripts.

Six Spelling Patterns Every OET Candidate Must Know

Instead of memorising every word individually, learn these six patterns. They cover the majority of OET medical vocabulary.

Pattern 1: -aemia (British)

  • anaemia
  • ischaemia
  • hypoglycaemia
  • septicaemia
  • uraemia

Pattern 2: -oea (British)

  • diarrhoea
  • dyspnoea
  • orthopnoea
  • apnoea
  • dyspnoea

Pattern 3: haemo- prefix

  • haemorrhage
  • haemoglobin
  • haemostasis
  • haemoptysis
  • haemodialysis

Pattern 4: -itis (inflammation)

  • appendicitis
  • tonsillitis
  • gastroenteritis
  • arthritis
  • bronchitis

Pattern 5: -oscopy (looking)

  • colonoscopy
  • bronchoscopy
  • laparoscopy
  • gastroscopy
  • arthroscopy

Pattern 6: -isation (British)

  • catheterisation
  • immunisation
  • sterilisation
  • hospitalisation
  • mobilisation
British vs American English in OET

OET accepts both British and American spellings — but be consistent throughout your writing. Don't write "anaemia" in one paragraph and "anemia" in the next. The examiners note inconsistency as a language control issue.

Memory Tricks for the Hardest Words

These are the words that consistently trip up even experienced healthcare professionals.

Haemorrhage

The most misspelled word in OET writing scripts. The key: one h, double r. Think of it as "haemo" (blood) + "rrhage" — rhage is like "rage", and when you're haemorrhaging, you're bleeding with rage. One h, two r's.

Diarrhoea

Candidates either forget the h (diarroea) or double the wrong letter (diarrhea). The British spelling: diarr + hoea. The "hoea" ending is the same as in dyspnoea and orthopnoea — learn it once, apply it everywhere.

Accommodation / Occurrence

Both follow the same rule: double the consonant twice. AccOmmodation has double c AND double m. OccUrrence has double c AND double r. A useful test — if you're unsure, double both consonants and you'll be right.

Necessary / Recommend

Necessary: one collar, two socks — 1 c, 2 s's. Recommend: one collar, two medals — 1 c, 2 m's. These appear constantly in OET referral letters ("I recommend... it is necessary to...").

Anaesthetic

Think: anaes + the + tic. The word contains "the" in the middle — anaes-THE-tic. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

From a doctor's experience

The words that caught me most in OET preparation were the ones I'd been writing in clinical shorthand for years — ECG, SOB, HTN. Spelling them out in full for OET felt unnatural. Practice writing full clinical terms in your notes for 4 weeks before your exam, and they'll feel natural on the day.

A 4-Week Spelling Study Strategy

Based on spaced repetition research and OET exam patterns.

Week 1 — Diagnose your weak spots

Take the 80 words in this guide and test yourself on all of them. Don't study yet — just identify which ones you already know and which ones you don't. Sort them into three piles: confident, uncertain, and no idea. Focus your energy on the "no idea" pile first.

Week 2 — Learn the patterns

Study the six spelling patterns above. Don't memorise individual words — memorise the pattern. If you know that British English uses -oea endings, you can correctly spell dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and apnoea without having ever specifically studied them.

Week 3 — Active recall practice

This is where a practice tool becomes essential. Reading a word and recognising it is much easier than reproducing it from memory under exam conditions. You need to practice writing words, not just reading them. Aim for 20-30 words per daily session, focusing on your weak spots.

Week 4 — Simulate exam conditions

Write complete OET-style referral letters and case notes, deliberately using the words you've been studying. Have a colleague or tutor check your spelling. The goal is for correct spelling to feel automatic, so you can focus your cognitive load on the clinical content of your writing.

Spaced repetition works

Research on language learning consistently shows that spacing your practice over time — rather than cramming — produces much stronger retention. Practicing 20 words daily for 4 weeks outperforms studying 560 words the night before the exam. Build the habit early.
