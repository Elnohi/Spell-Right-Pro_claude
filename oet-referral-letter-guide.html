What is the OET Writing Sub-test?

Understanding what you are actually being asked to produce.

The OET Writing sub-test gives you 45 minutes to produce a professional clinical letter based on case notes provided in the test. The letter is typically a referral letter, discharge letter, or transfer letter — written from one healthcare professional to another.

The case notes include patient demographics, medical history, test results, medications, and the reason for the referral or transfer. Your task is to extract the relevant information, organise it appropriately, and express it clearly in professional clinical English.

The target word count is approximately 180–200 words. Going significantly over or under this range can affect your score, though there is no strict penalty for word count itself.

What the examiner is looking for

OET Writing is NOT testing your clinical knowledge — it is testing your ability to communicate clinical information clearly, concisely, and in the appropriate professional register. You will not be penalised for clinical decisions. You will be penalised for unclear language, poor spelling, and inappropriate tone.

How OET Writing is Scored

The five criteria examiners use — and which matter most.

Purpose

Criterion 1

Is the purpose of the letter clear from the opening? Does the reader immediately understand why the letter has been written?

Content

Criterion 2 — heavily weighted

Have you included all the relevant information from the case notes? Have you excluded irrelevant details? Is the information accurate?

Conciseness & Clarity

Criterion 3

Is the letter free of unnecessary repetition? Is the information presented clearly and logically without ambiguity?

Layout & Organisation

Criterion 4

Does the letter follow a logical structure? Are paragraphs used appropriately? Is the overall layout professional?

Language

Criterion 5 — most heavily weighted

Is the vocabulary appropriate for clinical professional communication? Is grammar correct? Is spelling accurate? Is the register formal and professional throughout?

Language quality is the key to Band B

Candidates who fail to achieve Band B in Writing almost always do so because of Language quality — not because of missing content. Poor spelling, incorrect grammar, and informal register are the most common reasons for a C or C+ rather than a B. This is why spelling practice is essential OET preparation, not optional.

The Structure of a Referral Letter

Every OET referral letter follows a predictable structure. Learn it and apply it every time.

1. Date and Recipient

Start with today's date (right-aligned), then the recipient's details. In OET, you write to the role, not a specific named person unless the case notes specify one.

2. Subject Line / Re: line

Include the patient's name and date of birth, or name and relevant identifier. This is standard in clinical letters and examiners expect it.

3. Opening Paragraph — Purpose

State clearly who you are, who the patient is, and why you are writing. This should be two to three sentences at most. Do not bury the reason for referral in paragraph three.

4. Background / History

Relevant medical history, medications, allergies, and social history that the receiving clinician needs to know. Be selective — do not copy all case notes. Include what is relevant to this referral.

5. Current Presentation

The reason the patient is being referred now — symptoms, examination findings, investigation results. This is often the most important paragraph.

6. Request / Action Required

A clear statement of what you are asking the recipient to do — assessment, further investigation, management, follow-up.

7. Closing

Thank the recipient, offer to provide further information, and sign off professionally.

Annotated Example Letter

A worked example showing what a Band B referral letter looks like — with annotations.

12 June 2026

The Cardiologist
Cardiology Department
City General Hospital

Re: Mr James Harrison, DOB: 14 March 1958

✅ Date, recipient role, and Re: line all present — examiner can immediately identify patient and purpose

Dear Dr,

I am writing to refer Mr Harrison, a 68-year-old retired teacher, for urgent cardiology assessment. He presented to our clinic today with a three-week history of exertional chest pain and dyspnoea, which has been progressively worsening.

✅ Purpose clear in first two sentences — who, why, and urgency all stated

Mr Harrison has a background of hypertension (managed with amlodipine 10mg daily), hypercholesterolaemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He is a current smoker with a 30 pack-year history. He has no known drug allergies.

✅ Relevant background only — medications, risk factors, allergies. Note correct spelling of hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia, diabetes mellitus

On examination today, his blood pressure was 158/94 mmHg and pulse was 88 beats per minute and regular. A 12-lead electrocardiogram demonstrated ST-segment depression in leads V4-V6. His troponin I level was mildly elevated at 0.06 µg/L (normal <0.04 µg/L).

✅ Current findings presented clearly with values — electrocardiogram spelled in full for the letter

Given the clinical picture of possible acute coronary syndrome, I would be grateful if you could assess Mr Harrison as a matter of urgency, with a view to further investigation including coronary angiography if clinically indicated.

✅ Clear request — what you want the recipient to do, and why it is urgent

Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require any further information.

Yours sincerely,

Dr A. Smith
General Practitioner

Why this letter scores Band B

Purpose is clear in sentence one. All relevant information is included (background, examination, investigations). Irrelevant case note details are excluded. Language is formal, clinical, and accurately spelled throughout. Layout is professional with appropriate paragraph breaks.

10 Common OET Writing Mistakes to Avoid

These errors consistently separate Band C from Band B candidates.

1. Copying case notes verbatim

The examiner knows what the case notes say. Copying them word for word demonstrates no language ability. Paraphrase and summarise in your own clinical language.

2. Including irrelevant information

Not everything in the case notes belongs in the letter. A referral to a cardiologist does not need the patient's dental history. Select information relevant to the purpose of the letter.

3. Burying the purpose

The reason for writing should be in the first paragraph — ideally in the first sentence. Examiners assess purpose as a separate criterion. Do not make them search for it.

4. Informal language

Avoid contractions (don't, can't, hasn't), colloquial expressions, and casual phrasing. Clinical letters are formal professional documents.

5. Spelling errors

Spelling errors in medical terminology signal to the examiner that your clinical English is not at the required standard. This is the single most controllable factor in your Language score. Practise spelling the high-frequency medical words before your exam.

6. Incorrect tense

Use past tense for what happened (the patient presented, the investigation showed) and present tense for current status (he is currently taking, she remains symptomatic).

7. Missing patient identifiers

Always include patient name and date of birth in the Re: line. Some candidates forget this basic element.

8. No clear closing request

The recipient needs to know what you want them to do. A letter that ends without a clear action request is clinically incomplete and scores poorly on Purpose.

9. Over-long letters

OET Writing rewards conciseness. A 300-word letter full of repetition and copied case note material will score lower than a crisp 190-word letter covering all the relevant points.

10. Addressing the letter to "Dear Doctor"

Use "Dear Dr," (without a name unless specified) or the recipient's role. "Dear Doctor" without a comma or title is informal and grammatically incorrect in a professional letter.

Spelling in OET Writing

Why spelling practice is the highest-return preparation activity for OET Writing.

Of all the controllable factors in your OET Writing Language score, spelling is the one most improved by targeted practice. Grammar errors require understanding of complex rules. Vocabulary range develops slowly over years. But spelling 80 high-frequency medical words correctly is achievable in 3–4 weeks of consistent practice.

The words that trip up OET candidates most frequently are the ones that appear in nearly every clinical letter: haemorrhage, hypertension, dyspnoea, anticoagulant, electrocardiogram, deteriorating, approximately, recommendation. These are the same words you will write in every referral letter for the rest of your clinical career.
