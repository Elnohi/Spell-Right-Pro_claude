The 50 Medical Abbreviations Most Commonly Tested in OET

A complete reference for OET Listening and Writing — with full spellings, meanings, and the clinical context in which each abbreviation appears.

Compiled by a practising doctor and OET candidate · Updated June 2026

Why Abbreviations Matter in OET

Abbreviations appear in the case notes and in the listening recordings.

In OET Writing, the case notes you are given to work from will contain standard medical abbreviations. You need to know what they mean to accurately summarise the patient's history — and in many cases, you should write out the full term in your letter rather than use the abbreviation, because a referral letter to another professional should use complete clinical language.

In OET Listening, particularly Parts A and B, you will hear clinical consultations that include abbreviated terms. You may be required to write responses on forms that use these abbreviations as field labels. Understanding them is essential for correct answers.

In your OET letter — spell it out

While abbreviations are acceptable in clinical notes, your OET referral letter should generally spell out medical terms in full — particularly diagnoses, medications, and investigations. Write "electrocardiogram" not "ECG", "myocardial infarction" not "MI", "shortness of breath" not "SOB". This demonstrates language quality to the examiner.

How to Study Abbreviations for OET

The best approach is not to memorise abbreviations in isolation — it is to associate each abbreviation with its full spelled-out form, because in OET Writing you will need to write the full term. Every time you see "MI" in case notes, mentally expand it to "myocardial infarction" and practise spelling the full term.

For OET Listening, the abbreviations tend to appear as form field labels. Practise filling in clinical forms using standard medical abbreviations as headers, so the format feels familiar in the exam.

A practical habit

For the 4 weeks before your OET exam, whenever you write clinical notes or documentation at work, challenge yourself to spell out abbreviations in full. Write "intravenous" instead of "IV", "twice daily" instead of "BD". This trains your brain to produce the full correct spelling automatically — which is exactly what OET Writing demands.
